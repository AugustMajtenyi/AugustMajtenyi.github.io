The Relationship between
Happiness and Immigration


Context: This informal study demonstrates my learning R and RStudio.

Objective: Use disparate datasets to identify trends and correlations in Immigration and happiness.

Links to the Datasets: World Happiness Reports (2015-2019, and 2020)
and corresponding Immigration Data


Combining the Data

The files were not easily transferrable. A had to manually edit a handful country/region names. The merged dataframe contains 899 observations of 157 unique countries representing
10 regions around the world. Below is a quick representation of the data:


Note: There is a overall rise in Median Happiness Scores from year-to-year (Yay!)

Initial Evaluation

The Charts show just about what you'd expect:
Immigrants are going to 'happier' countries and emigrating from less happy countries. From this trend, it follows that 'happier' countries also have higher counts of Rejecting Immigrants.


Let's go deeper


The Ratio of Accepted vs. Rejected Immmigration Decisions



Analysis

Overall, there is a negative correlation (r = -0.192) between Immigration Acceptance Percentage and the World Happiness Scores for countries.


The scatterplot by region was particularly interesting to me. The 3 lines of regression at the top stand out from the other regions. From top to bottom respectively, they belong to Australia and New Zealand, North America, and Western Europe. These happen to also be the only three regions where all countries have positive migration population.
This me leads to a new question...


Is there a significant relationship between Happiness and Immigration Acceptance Ratio with Positive Migration Regions?


Positive, but not Certain


From the scatterlplot above, we see a positive correlation between Immigration Acceptance Ratio and Happiness, but with a pearson correlation of r = .107, it is not significant (p=.203)


Finding Significance



There is however, a significant correlation to be found. Generosity Score, which is determined by how much a country donates to charity, has a p-value of .006 (shown from the histogram above)



Reflection

The hidden bias of data analysis

I found a correlation that was significant, but at what cost? The original data showed a negative correlation, but I narrowed the data and found a positive one. Was it justified? I'm sure I could easily have analyzed the same data in a different way to find a totally different conclusion.


Research Limitations

Continuing this research with different recent years could test the validity of the trends I found. Also, region organization greatly impacted on my findings (Ex: Mexico was not in North America). This categorization is from the World Happiness Report; I'm not sure how they decided this.


Next Steps

I would like to develop my skills in R/Rstudio. I would like exposure to different packages and control structures. Coming back to this data after gaining more experience could prove useful.
